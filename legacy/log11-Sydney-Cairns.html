Weĺd always planned a stop somewhere in the Whitsundays and decided on Hamilton Island since itĺs only a little way out of our way.  It became clear that we were going to get there in the wee hours and where possible, we never come into a strange Marina at night.  So we headed offshore for an hour and then hove to ľ this is a great trick to stall the yacht and has a very comfortable motion.  Since we werenĺt going anywhere, Pommy took on my watch and I managed to grab a few hours sleep ľ wonderful.

After a couple of days, the forecast for the next day looked good so we got ready to leave ľ alarms being set for 5am.  And they would have worked if we hadnĺt already been awake listening to the wind howling though the rigging and the rain bucketing down.  That day, I went and had a chat with an old guy who runs one the yacht charter firms to get a bit of local knowledge.  Not a happy bunny with 20 odd yachts either sitting in the Marina or hiding in safe anchorages (that equates to some 100 odd holiday makers who were not enjoying their expensive charters).

 

He said heĺd never seen weather like this at this time of year in the 10 odd years heĺd worked there and had no idea what the weather was going to do.  He advised staying for another 48 hours.

 

But the next morning dawned with a hint of sun, nice winds and no rain ľ so we went.  We should have listened.  At first it was grand sailing, but as the day wore on the wind increased.  By midnight, we had constant 35-40 knot winds, gusting up to 45 ľ a full gale.  We had the deepest reefs we could in the main and mizzen and a handkerchief for a foresail and it was still a pretty wild ride.

The plan was to only spend 48 hours here, but itĺs drifted out to five days.  With all the bad weather stops, weĺre so far behind schedule that weĺre going to probably miss the start of the rally up to Indonesia anyway, but weĺve learned thereĺs a second group leaving two weeks later that weĺll either join or slot in between them and the main rally.

Weĺve also spent a day and more going through all the charts from here to Darwin and working out the route.  This is going to be the longest leg yet to date ľ almost 1,300nm (c.1,500 miles) up to Cape York and then across the Gulf of Carpentaria. 

 

Itĺs going to be challenging.

 

The first 500nm are all up inside the Great Barrier Reef, dodging bits of coral and the big ships that use the very few clear passages.  Most yachts do this in day-by-day bites, anchoring overnight, but we have to go straight through.  To add a certain piquant, the Pilot Books all say you can be pretty certain of a nice 20-25kn SE wind at this time of year ľ great for us ľ but the forecast is for 5-10kn ľ looks like weĺll be breaking out all the light wind sails weĺve not had a chance to play with yet.  And we only carry enough fuel to motor maybe 400 miles and there are few places to refuel.

And, at last, we have to start thinking about tides and currents again.  In the early part of the trip north, we had to try and keep out of the strong southerly flow of the East Australian Current, but that was more a matter of watching the sea temperature  - if it went up, we were in the current being knocked back by a few knots so weĺd just head further inshore.  From here on, the tides increase (up to 3 meters around Cape York and a mind boggling 6 or 7 meters in Darwin ľ imagine the old family holidays at St Maryĺs Bay with a 25 foot tide).  Some of the tides/currents weĺll meet will mean waiting for the right time to go through otherwise theyĺll push us back faster than we can go forwards.

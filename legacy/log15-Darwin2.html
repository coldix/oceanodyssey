Darwin II

 

 

17th October 2007

Written by Peter 

 

Greetings All,

 

And a special Happy Birthday to Mum.

 

I think today is the 3rd month anniversary of arriving in Darwin ľ and weĺre starting to slip into the life-style here.  Darwin is part of the Northern Territory, or NT as it is abbreviated.  It appears that NT also stands for ôNot Todayö ôNot Tomorrowöľ itĺs a sort of Aussie version of ôma˝anaö ľ why do something today or even tomorrow when you can put it off until some other time!

 

A lot of this is down to the weather.  While we are only at the start of the ôBuild-upö (the season of change between the dry, although still hot winter months, known as "The Dry", and the monsoonal months of ôThe Wetö), there has been a noticeable increase in the humidity.  Weĺre told, almost on a daily basis, by the locals that this is nothing yet and it will get a lot worse over the next month or so.  I donĺt know if it could ľ I literally already have the sweat dripping off me the whole time during the day ľ the night is a bit better and our greatest fear is that when weĺre told it will get worse, they mean the night humidity will go through the roof.

 

On the positive side though, weĺre back on ôHinewaiö who is now moored in Tipperary Waters Marina on the southern side of Darwin.  The French boat that was in our berth promised us that they would definitely be gone by the 20th Sept so we were able to promise Julie her flat back that weekend.  We should have known ľ the Frenchies did not go, but promised they would be gone by the following Wednesday.  Julie had been so wonderful letting us use the flat but it didnĺt seem fair to stay on after weĺd committed to leave so we ended up moving into ôThe Cavanaghö, one of the backpacker hotels in town, for a few days.  Basic, but clean, good food, nice bar and a grand air con unit in the room.

 

On the Wednesday, Jean popped down to Tipperary to see the French again ľ ôMais nonö they said, they are still not going.  Peter, the marina manager, then suggested we bring Hinewai in to a spare berth anyway ľ it was too small for us to stay in, but we could then move once the Frenchies had gone.

 

So the next day, Jean rustled up a few friends and they brought the big girl round.  Now while we are still in the early stages of the build up, we have had the odd bit of rain ľ and that Thursday was the first really big rain weĺd seen.  About 2ö fell in under an hour.  It looked very spectacular from the bar of the Sailing Club where I was sitting ľ Jean and the others, who by that time had just raised the anchor and were motoring round, were not so impressed.

 

Once the rain had stopped, I drove round to Tipperary and found a few other yachties to help catch the lines and berth the yacht as she came in.

 

Four days later, we finally waved fond ôAdieuö to the Frenchies and moved the boat to our current berth.

 

And set about trying to get the big girl set up so we could survive the next few months.

 

The first job was to get the air-conditioner installed.  This is one of those old through wall units which we picked up second hand from ôCash Convertersö ľ the modern Aussie version of a Pawn Shop.  Itĺs set up on the deck next to the saloon hatch with a couple of aluminiumised car sun-screens cut up into the ducting to get the cool air down below.  And, even though itĺs a very small unit for the space down below, it's working a dream.  Itĺs only dropping the temperature by a couple of degrees, but the big thing is itĺs taking the humidity out of the air.

 

Then to get the awnings up.  These are also known as ôBoom Tentsö since they often use the booms like the centre pole of a tent.  Our Mizzen awning is a simple boom tent, but the main is more complicated and shaped, being held up by the main halyard to give a grand big area underneath.  And once weĺd worked out that we were trying to put it on back to front, it fits really well.

 

Tipperary is not a bad Marina ľ based upon an old creek which has been widened, dredged out and developed with houses all around it and a lock to handle the big tides.  It has the usual facilities of shore power, loos & showers [only 160 steps to get there ľ some forethought is handy] and laundry (although the loo and  laundrycould be cleaner), but itĺs not cheap.  Just to park the boat is $405 per 28 day period.  Then add $7.50 per day liveaboard charge AND $4.50 per day to run the air-con and weĺre soon paying over $900 a month.

 

Most of the boats moored here have liveaboards ľ people who live on their boats.  Some live here in Darwin full time, but most are simply staying to ride out the cyclone season and in many cases of the foreigners, to travel round Oz.  Thereĺs still a couple of boats planning to go soon ľ either heading over to Asia or back down the East Coast - but itĺs pretty close to the end of the safe period to do so.  But the night before anyone leaves, we all get together at the top end of the Marina for a few drinks ľ a good way to meet our neighbours. (One boat has now had three attempts at leaving ľ each time something has broken and theyĺve had to return.  But Iĺm sure weĺll see them off with a few drinks again).

 

Now we are here and settled, weĺve been turning our thoughts to what weĺll do over the next few months.

Getting a job for either of us is going to be interesting up here.  In fact, we are going to have to make a decision about what we want to do.

 

Thereĺs loads of lower level jobs ľ bars, hospitality etc ľ but the payĺs pretty dire.  If I were fit, we might look at getting work on one of the mines (they are crying out for drivers etc), but then if I was fit, we wouldnĺt be here.

 

Jeanĺs actually just finished a two week introductory course on commercial cooking.  Since it was free, she did it as much to fill time, but it looks like it may open a few doors to getting ôFront of House/Hotel Managementö opportunities ľ and that would be a skill she be able to carry with her around the world.  So in some ways it might be a reasonable investment in her time.

 

Unfortunately, thereĺs no call for the sort of work we did down south because the market is so small up here ľ most advertising DM is handled by southern agencies if itĺs of any size ľ indeed, thereĺs only one ad agency up here.  Indeed, in many ways, weĺre too qualified to even look for short term work in our field, but weĺve spoken with a couple of employment agencies up here (there are no executive ôhead-hunterö type organisations) and will let them have our CVĺs.  (They do love their job titles up here ľ in last weekend paper, there was a position for a ôDirector of First Impressionsö ľ reading further, itĺs for a receptionist).

 

Another option is for one of us to head south for a few months and it would make sense for this to be Jean going back to Perth to be with her family as well.

 

So itĺs all a little up in the air at the moment and a little hard to make a decision until Iĺve seen the Specialist on Friday.

 

In the meantime, at least we can get around Darwin more easily now that we have bought a car.  This needed another set of decisions.  Since we will only need it for a few months, how could we minimise how much weĺll loose on it?  Obviously, buying a new car would be daft ľ weĺd drop $3,000 as soon as we drove off the lot, but should we buy a really cheap old car? We might not loose too much on when we sell (or even throw away) but it might cost a bit to keep running or not be very reliable.  Or a good second hander?

 

We spent a week looking around and ended up buying a 12 month old Hyundai Getz.  Its mileage is quite high at around 30k on the clock, but that meant it was a lot cheaper at $10,300 than most.  Since we donĺt plan to do too many miles, we hope the mileage will even out when we sell it ľ and with the Getz being very popular small car at the moment we shouldnĺt loose too much.

 

The downside is that it is bright yellow and while Jean enjoys driving round in her Noddy Car, itĺs not doing my street cred too much good.

 

Buying the car was the easy bit though.  Then we had to endure the stupidity of Australiaĺs State government bureaucracies.

 

Here we are, two Victorians temporarily living in the NT with no fixed abode (a boat is not considered an abode) trying to transfer ownership of a NT registered car to our Victorian based company.  There was that intake of breath and ôitĺs more than my jobs worthö when we arrived at the RTA office. At first we were told we had to have a registered office in Darwin (and thatĺs not so).  Then that we had to have bank accounts up here (also not so).  Eventually, we compromised at providing copies of our company documents proving that we were in fact the directors of the Company (donĺt ask me why that was needed) and a bill showing an address in Darwin (The Marina is 80 Frances Bay Road so we fudged that one).

 

We were relieved that no-one mentioned our Victorian driving licences.  Technically, we should surrender our Vic DLĺs after 3 months with a car here and get NT licences ľ at, of course, a cost of $50 each.  But we hope we wonĺt have to worry about this since weĺll both be going interstate before that time is up when we head back to Perth for Xmas and then weĺll start the 3 months again.

 

But, on the other hand, weĺre not able to vote up here.  As you may have heard, our poisonous lying git of a PM has finally run out of time and, even with his appalling ratings in the polls, has had to call an election.  While the NT is trying to get us to ôbeö here for the car, with no fixed abode we canĺt vote here.  So itĺs going to be a postal vote back to our old electorate.

 

No wonder we all pay so much tax trying to support this idiocy of all the levels of Federal, State and Local bureaucracies.

 

One final observation.  Thereĺs very little in the papers or on TV at the moment about the Rugby World Cup.  I wonder why after our boysĺ magnificent performance a couple of weeks ago (and that magic game against the Frenchies last weekend).

 

The final next Sunday morning (weĺll be watching about 4am our time) is going to be interesting.  Of course, Iĺll be supporting Johnny and the other 14 incidentals while young Jean seems to be having a throw back to her darkest Africa days and will be supporting the Boks.  We hope our neighbours donĺt want to sleep too much that night.

 

Finally, weĺve just about got the website up to date with news and picccies.

 

Thatĺs about all for now, so Iĺll get this off.
